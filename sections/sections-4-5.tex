We now turn from projective space to the wider world of toric varieties, whose combinatorial description makes them an ideal candidate for scrutinizing the structure of derived categories. Throughout, $N\cong\ZZ^d$ is the lattice of \defi{one-parameter subgroups} of a torus $T = N\otimes_\ZZ \kk^*\cong(\kk^*)^d$ and its dual $M=\Hom_\ZZ(N,\ZZ)$ is the \defi{character lattice} of $T$. The usual, somewhat cryptic incantation is: a \defi{toric variety} is a normal variety $X$ containing $T$ as a dense open subset, together with an action of $T$ on $X$ extending its action on itself. The next two lectures focus on their combinatorial and algebraic structure instead. Our references are \cite[Ch.~3--5]{CLS} and David Cox's paper \cite{cox}.

\subsection{Toric varieties from fans}

An \defi{affine} toric variety is built from a cone. Let $\sigma\subseteq N\otimes\RR$ be a strongly convex rational polyhedral cone, with dual cone $\sigma^\vee\subseteq M\otimes\RR$. Then $S_\sigma=\sigma^\vee\cap M$ is a finitely generated semigroup, and $U_\sigma=\Spec \kk[S_\sigma]$ is an affine toric variety. Using these affine pieces, an arbitrary normal toric variety is glued together along the combinatorics of a \defi{fan}.

\begin{defn}
  A \defi{fan} $\Sigma$ in $N\otimes\RR$ is a finite collection of strongly convex rational polyhedral cones such that (i) every face of a cone in $\Sigma$ is again in $\Sigma$, and (ii) the intersection of any two cones in $\Sigma$ is a face of each. The associated toric variety $X_\Sigma$ is obtained by gluing the affine charts $\{U_\sigma\}_{\sigma\in\Sigma}$ along the charts of their common faces.
\end{defn}

The dictionary between the geometry of $X_\Sigma$ and combinatorics of $\Sigma$ is explained in \cite[\S3.1]{CLS}. In particular $X_\Sigma$ is \defi{smooth} (resp.\ \defi{simplicial}) if every cone of $\Sigma$ is generated by part of a $\ZZ$-basis (resp.\ an $\RR$-basis) of $N$, and it is \defi{complete} (equivalently proper) exactly when $\Sigma$ covers all of $N\otimes\RR$. We assume from now on that the rays of $\Sigma$ span $N\otimes\RR$, i.e.\ that $X_\Sigma$ has \defi{no torus factor}; this is automatic when $X_\Sigma$ is complete, as all of our examples are.

\subsection{Rays, divisors, and the class group}

Write $\Sigma(1)$ for the set of \defi{rays} (one-dimensional cones) of $\Sigma$, and let $u_\rho\in N$ denote the primitive generator of a ray $\rho$. By the Orbit--Cone Correspondence \cite[\S3.2]{CLS}, each ray $\rho$ corresponds to an irreducible $T$-invariant Weil divisor $D_\rho$ on $X_\Sigma$, and every $T$-invariant divisor is an integral combination $\sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$. Identifying $\Div_T(X_\Sigma)=\ZZ^{\Sigma(1)}$, there is an exact sequence \cite[Thm.~4.1.3]{CLS}
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:SEScl4}
  0\to M\xrightarrow{\ \alpha\ } \ZZ^{\Sigma(1)}\xrightarrow{\ \deg\ } \Cl(X_\Sigma)\to 0,
  \qquad \alpha(m)=\sum_\rho \langle m,-u_\rho\rangle D_\rho,
\end{equation}
\michael{Beware: I introduced a sign on $u_\rho$ above, to match Section 6}
where $\Cl(X_\Sigma)$ is the \defi{divisor class group}. The map $\alpha$ gives the principal divisor of the character $\chi^m$, and $\deg$ sends a divisor to its class; injectivity of $\alpha$ uses our assumption that the rays span $N\otimes\RR$.

\begin{remark}[Cartier and Weil divisors]\label{rem:PicCl}
  Inside the class group sits the Picard group $\Pic(X_\Sigma) \xhookrightarrow{} \Cl(X_\Sigma)$ of \emph{Cartier} divisor classes, and the gap between the two measures the singularities of $X_\Sigma$. The toric variety is smooth if and only if $\Pic(X_\Sigma) = \Cl(X_\Sigma)$ (note that isomorphism is not sufficient), and it is simplicial if and only if $\Pic(X_\Sigma)$ has finite index in $\Cl(X_\Sigma)$, in which case every Weil divisor is \defi{$\QQ$-Cartier}, meaning some positive multiple is Cartier \cite[Prop.~4.2.6-7]{CLS}. Example~\ref{ex:P112charts} below illustrates both phenomena on $\PP(1,1,2)$; the distinction is also apparent in the module--sheaf correspondence and in Demazure vanishing in Lecture~\ref{lec:5-toric-cox}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Cox ring and irrelevant ideal}

Assign to each ray $\rho\in\Sigma(1)$ a variable $x_\rho$ and consider
\[ S = \Cox(X_\Sigma) = \kk[x_\rho : \rho\in\Sigma(1)] \]
to be a polynomial ring graded by $\Cl(X_\Sigma)$ via $\deg(x_\rho)=[D_\rho]$. This is the \defi{Cox ring} (or \defi{homogeneous coordinate ring}) of $X_\Sigma$. A monomial $x^{\mathbf a}=\prod_\rho x_\rho^{a_\rho}$ has degree $[\sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho]\in\Cl(X_\Sigma)$.

The geometry of $X_\Sigma$, encoded in the combinatorics of the maximal cones of $\Sigma$, is recorded by the so-called \defi{irrelevant ideal}
\[
B = \bigl\langle\, x^{\widehat\sigma}\ :\ \sigma\in\Sigma_{\max}\,\bigr\rangle, \qquad
x^{\widehat\sigma} = \prod_{\rho\;\notin\;\sigma(1)} x_\rho,
\]
which is a squarefree monomial ideal generated, for each maximal cone $\sigma$, by the product of the variables whose rays are not in $\sigma$.

\begin{remark}[Primitive collections]\label{rem:primitive}
  The locus $V(B)\subseteq\bA^{\Sigma(1)}$ cut out by the irrelevant ideal is a union of coordinate subspaces, indexed by the \defi{primitive collections} of $\Sigma$: the minimal subsets $\mathcal C\subseteq\Sigma(1)$ that do not span a cone of $\Sigma$, although every proper subset does. Explicitly,
  \[ V(B) = \bigcup_{\mathcal C} V(x_\rho : \rho\in\mathcal C) \quad \text{\cite[Prop.~5.1.6]{CLS}}. \]
  Observe that the ring $S$, its grading, and the group $G$ below depend only on the rays $\Sigma(1)$, whereas $B$ and $V(B)$ remember the maximal cones; in particular, different fans with the same rays share the Cox ring $S$ but not the ideal $B$. We return to this observation in Lecture~\ref{lec:5-toric-cox} and, systematically, in Lecture~\ref{lec:11-secondary-fan}.
\end{remark}

\begin{thm}[quotient construction, \cite{CLS}*{Thm.~5.1.11}]\label{thm:coxquotient}
  Let $X_\Sigma$ be a toric variety with no torus factor and let $G=\Hom_\ZZ(\Cl(X_\Sigma),\kk^*)$ act on $\Spec S=\bA^{\Sigma(1)}$ via the grading on $S$. Then
  \[ X_\Sigma \cong \bigl(\Spec S\setminus V(B)\bigr)\,/\!\!/\,G \]
  is a good categorical quotient, and it is a geometric quotient if and only if $X_\Sigma$ is simplicial.
\end{thm}

Thus a point of a simplicial $X_\Sigma$ is a $G$-orbit of ``homogeneous coordinates'' $[a_\rho]_{\rho\in\Sigma(1)}$ not lying in the excluded locus $V(B)$. For $\PP^n$ this recovers the familiar $\PP^n=(\bA^{n+1}\setminus\{0\})\,/\,\kk^*$.

\begin{example}\label{ex:toricrunning}
  We now illustrate several toric surfaces as running examples.
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Projective space $\PP^n$.}  The fan has as rays the standard basis $u_1,\dots,u_n$ and their complement $u_0 = -u_1-\dots-u_n$, with maximal cones spanned by all $n$-subsets. Then $S = \kk[x_0,\dots,x_n]$ with the standard $\ZZ$-grading $\deg(x_i) = 1$ and $B = (x_0,\dots,x_n)$: the only primitive collection is all of $\Sigma(1)$, so $V(B) = \{0\}$.

  \item \textbf{Products $\PP^{n_1}\times\dots\times\PP^{n_r}$.}  The Cox ring is a multigraded polynomial ring with $\ZZ^r$-grading and $B = \bigcap_i (x_{i,0},\dots,x_{i,n_i})$; the primitive collections are the $k$ sets of rays of the factors.

  \item \textbf{Weighted projective space $\PP(a_0,\dots,a_n)$, where $\gcd(a_0,\dots,a_n) = 1$.}  The fan is given by the quotient lattice $N = \ZZ^{n+1}/(a_0,\dots,a_n)$, the rays being the images of the standard basis vectors. Then, $S = \kk[x_0,\dots,x_n]$ and $B = (x_0,\dots,x_n)$ with $\deg(x_i) = a_i$. Note that the inclusion of the Picard group in the class group is $\on{lcm}(a_0,\dots,a_n)\ZZ \xhookrightarrow{} \ZZ$, thus weighted projective spaces are generally singular; see Example~\ref{ex:P112charts}.

  \item \textbf{Hirzebruch surfaces $\mathcal H_a$ ($a\ge 1$).}  The surface $\mathcal H_a=\PP(\cO_{\PP^1}\oplus\cO_{\PP^1}(a))$ is the toric variety of the fan in $N=\ZZ^2$ with rays
    \[
    u_0=e_1,\qquad u_1=e_2,\qquad u_2=-e_1+ae_2,\qquad u_3=-e_2,
    \]
    and the four maximal cones spanned by neighbouring pairs (Figure~\ref{fig:fans}). Writing $x_i$ for the variable of $u_i$, the sequence \eqref{eqn:SEScl4} gives the relations $D_0\sim D_2$ and $D_3\sim D_1+aD_2$, so in the basis $([D_2],[D_3])$ of $\Cl(\mathcal H_a)=\ZZ^2$ the degrees are
    \[
    \deg(x_0)=\deg(x_2)=(1,0),\qquad \deg(x_1)=(-a,1),\qquad \deg(x_3)=(0,1),
    \]
    and the primitive collections $\{u_0,u_2\}$ and $\{u_1,u_3\}$ give $B=(x_0,x_2)\cap(x_1,x_3)$. Geometrically, $(1,0)$ is the class of a fiber of $\mathcal H_a\to\PP^1$ and class $(-a,1)$ is the class of the divisor with self-intersection $-a$.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{example}

\begin{example}[$\PP(1,1,2)$ in detail]\label{ex:P112charts}
  Let $S = \kk[x,y,z]$ with $\deg(x)=\deg(y)=1$ and $\deg(z)=2$ and $B = (x,y,z)$. By Theorem~\ref{thm:coxquotient}, $\PP(1,1,2) = (\bA^3\setminus\{0\})\,/\,\kk^*$ for the action $\lambda\cdot(x,y,z)=(\lambda x,\lambda y,\lambda^2 z)$. The irrelevant ideal gives the open affine cover $D_+(x)\cup D_+(y)\cup D_+(z)$, where
  \[ D_+(x) = \Spec\bigl(S[x^{-1}]\bigr)_0 = \Spec \kk\Bigl[\tfrac{y}{x},\tfrac{z}{x^2}\Bigr]\cong\bA^2, \]
  and analogously for $D_+(y)\cong\bA^2$, but
  \[ D_+(z) = \Spec\bigl(S[z^{-1}]\bigr)_0 = \Spec\kk\Bigl[\tfrac{x^2}{z},\tfrac{xy}{z},\tfrac{y^2}{z}\Bigr]\cong \Spec \kk[u,v,w]/(uw-v^2) \]
  is the quadric cone. Thus $\PP(1,1,2)$ is singular exactly at the point $[0:0:1]$, where the quotient has stabilizer $\mu_2 = \{\pm1\}$. In particular, a divisor class $d\in\Cl(\PP(1,1,2)) = \ZZ$ is Cartier if and only if $d$ is a multiple of $2 = \on{lcm}(1,1,2)$, so $\Pic(\PP(1,1,2)) = 2\ZZ \subsetneq \ZZ$, as Remark~\ref{rem:PicCl} predicts for a simplicial but non-smooth toric variety. In particular the rank-one reflexive sheaf $\cO(1)$, corresponding to the module $S(1)$, is \emph{not} a line bundle. This point is further scruitinized in Lecture~\ref{lec:5-toric-cox}.
\end{example}

\subsection{The ideal--variety correspondence}

Just as homogeneous ideals of $\kk[x_0,\dots,x_n]$ cut out closed subschemes of $\PP^n$, homogeneous ideals of $S$ cut out closed subschemes of $X$, provided we account for the irrelevant ideal $B$. Call a homogeneous ideal $I\subseteq S$ \defi{$B$-saturated} if
\[ I = I : B^\infty = \{f\in S : B^k f\subseteq I \text{ for some } k\}. \]

\begin{remark}[Saturation and local cohomology]\label{rem:localcohomology}
  Both notions of $B$-saturation are governed by \defi{local cohomology}. For a $\Cl(X)$-graded $S$-module $M$, its \defi{$B$-torsion submodule}
  \[ H^0_B(M) = \{\,m\in M : B^k m = 0 \text{ for some } k\ge 0\,\} \]
  is the largest submodule supported on $V(B)$, and $H^i_B(M)$ denotes its higher right-derived functors, computed from the \v{C}ech complex on a generating set of $B$ \cite[\S9]{CLS}. The bridge to geometry\footnote{This reflects the geometry when $X$ is smooth, and in the simplicial case after replacing $X$ by its canonical stack $\sX$ of Theorem~\ref{thm:simplicialStack}.} is the four-term exact sequence of $\Cl(X)$-graded modules
  \[
  0 \longrightarrow H^0_B(M) \longrightarrow M \longrightarrow \Gamma_{\!*}(\widetilde M) \longrightarrow H^1_B(M) \longrightarrow 0, \qquad
  \Gamma_{\!*}(\widetilde M) \ce \bigoplus_{\mathbf d\in\Cl(X)} H^0\bigl(X,\widetilde{M(\mathbf d)}\bigr),
  \]
  together with the isomorphisms $H^p\bigl(X,\widetilde{M(\mathbf d)}\bigr) \cong H^{p+1}_B(M)_{\mathbf d}$ for $p\ge 1$. Thus $H^0_B(M)$ is exactly the part of $M$ that sheafification forgets, while $H^1_B(M)$ measures the failure of $M$ to already consist of all twisted global sections. Passing to $\Gamma_{\!*}(\widetilde M)$ kills both, thus a module $M$ is called \defi{$B$-saturated} when $H^0_B(M) = H^1_B(M) = 0$. An ideal $I\subseteq S$ is $B$-saturated in this sense precisely when $H^1_B(I) = H^0_B(S/I) = (I : B^\infty) / I = 0$, since $H^0_B(I) = 0$ is automatic.

  Among all finitely generated modules with a given sheaf, or all ideals cutting out a given subscheme, the saturated one is the canonical choice, which is why the correspondences below are phrased through $B$-saturated representatives.
\end{remark}

\begin{prop}[Toric Ideal--Variety Correspondence, \cite{CLS}*{Prop.~5.2.7}]\label{prop:ideal-variety}
  Let $X$ be a simplicial toric variety with Cox ring $S$ and irrelevant ideal $B$. Then there is a bijective correspondence
  \[ \{\text{closed subvarieties of } X\} \longleftrightarrow \{\text{radical homogeneous ideals } I\subseteq B\}. \]
\end{prop}

Here CLS normalizes a radical ideal by intersecting with $B$; replacing $I$ by its $B$-saturation instead gives the equivalent normalization by radical $B$-saturated ideals, matching the classical statement for $\PP^n$. At the level of subschemes the correspondence requires smoothness:

\begin{prop}[\cite{CLS}*{Prop.~6.A.7}]\label{prop:ideal-sheaf}
  On a smooth toric variety $X$, two homogeneous ideals $I,J\subseteq S$ determine the same ideal sheaf (hence the same closed subscheme) if and only if $(I:B^\infty)=(J:B^\infty)$. Consequently, closed subschemes of $X$ correspond bijectively to $B$-saturated homogeneous ideals of $S$.
\end{prop}

\subsection{The Cox ring does not remember the variety}

A subtlety absent from projective space is that the Cox ring is not a complete invariant: as observed in Remark~\ref{rem:primitive}, the ring $S$, its grading, and the group $G$ depend only on the rays of $\Sigma$, while the irrelevant ideal remembers the maximal cones.

\begin{example}\label{ex:sameCox}
  Let $S=\kk[x_0,x_1,x_2,x_3]$ carry the $\ZZ^2$-grading of Example~\ref{ex:toricrunning}(4) with $a=3$, so that
  \[ \deg(x_0)=\deg(x_2)=(1,0),\qquad \deg(x_1)=(-3,1),\qquad \deg(x_3)=(0,1), \]
  and let $G = (\kk^*)^2$ act accordingly. Two different squarefree monomial ideals yield two very different geometric quotients:
  \begin{itemize}
  \item $B = (x_0,x_2) \cap (x_1,x_3)$ yields the Hirzebruch surface $\mathcal H_3$, which is smooth;
  \item $B'= (x_1) \cap (x_0,x_2,x_3)$ yields the weighted projective space $\PP(1,1,3)$, which is singular: on the locus $x_1 \neq 0$ we may use part of $G$ to scale $x_1$ to $1$, and the residual $\kk^* = \{(s, s^3)\} \subseteq G$ acts on $(x_0,x_2,x_3)$ with weights $(1,1,3)$, exhibiting the quotient as $(\bA^3\setminus\{0\})\,/\,\kk^* = \PP(1,1,3)$.
  \end{itemize}
  Since the two quotients are not isomorphic, the Cox ring together with the group action does not determine the variety: the irrelevant ideal is essential extra data. Beware, however, that only the first presentation is the Cox construction of Theorem~\ref{thm:coxquotient}: the Cox ring of $\PP(1,1,3)$ itself is the \emph{three}-variable ring $\kk[y_0,y_1,y_2]$ with $\Cl(\PP(1,1,3))=\ZZ$ and degrees $(1,1,3)$, because the ray corresponding to $x_1$ does not appear in its fan; accordingly, $x_1$ generates a principal component of $B'$. This is the first hint of the \defi{secondary fan}, which parametrizes all the toric varieties arising from a fixed graded polynomial ring; see Example~\ref{ex:hirzsecondary} and Remark~\ref{rmk:abuseNotation} in Lecture~\ref{lec:11-secondary-fan}, and Lecture~\ref{lec:12-DCox-definition} for the derived category incarnation of this same pair of quotients.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}
  To reconstruct $X_\Sigma$ from the Cox ring $S$, one may specify any one of the following, each of which determines the others:
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item\label{item:max} the choice of maximal cones in $\Sigma_X$; % how to compactify the torus
  \item\label{item:irr} the choice of an irrelevant ideal $B\subset S$;
  \item\label{item:nef} the choice of the nef cone of $X_\Sigma$ inside the secondary fan.
  %\item\label{item:ext}the choice of higher extensions between the Frobenius line bundles.
  \end{enumerate}
  We will return to this in Lecture~\ref{lec:11-secondary-fan}, where we will see that the chambers in the secondary fan precisely enumerate the possible choices for each of the above.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Modules to sheaves}

Every finitely generated graded $S$-module $M$ gives rise to a coherent sheaf $\widetilde M$ on $X$, following \cite[\S5.3]{CLS}. On the affine chart $U_\sigma$ associated to a maximal cone $\sigma$, the sections are the degree-zero part of the localization at the monomial $x^{\widehat\sigma}=\prod_{\rho\,\notin\,\sigma(1)}x_\rho$:
\[ \Gamma\bigl(U_\sigma,\widetilde M\bigr)=\bigl(M[\,x^{-\widehat\sigma}\,]\bigr)_0. \]
These glue to a sheaf $\widetilde M$ on $X$, and $M\mapsto\widetilde M$ is exact. For a class $\mathbf d\in\Cl(X)$, the module $S(\mathbf d)$ sheafifies to the rank-one reflexive sheaf $\cO_X(\mathbf d)=\widetilde{S(\mathbf d)}$.

\begin{remark}\label{rem:twistcaveat}
  When $X$ is smooth, the sheaves $\cO_X(\mathbf d)$ are line bundles, $\cO(\mathbf d)\otimes\cO(\mathbf e)\cong\cO(\mathbf d+\mathbf e)$, and twisting commutes with sheafification: $\widetilde{M(\mathbf d)}\cong\widetilde M\otimes\cO(\mathbf d)$. All three statements may fail for classes outside $\Pic(X)$ when $X$ is merely simplicial: on $\PP(1,1,2)$ the sheaf $\cO(1)$ is not a line bundle (Example~\ref{ex:P112charts}), and the natural map $\cO(1)\otimes\cO(1)\to\cO(2)$ is not an isomorphism. Handling such twists correctly is one motivation for the toric stacks introduced below.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The derived equivalence and its failure}
The kernel of the (exact) sheafification functor is what separates the geometry of $X$ from the algebra of $S$. Write $\Mod_{\mathrm{gr}}(S)$ for the category of finitely generated $\Cl(X)$-graded $S$-modules, and $\Mod_B(S)\subseteq\Mod_{\mathrm{gr}}(S)$ for the full subcategory of \defi{$B$-torsion} modules, i.e.\ those annihilated by a power of $B$.

\begin{prop}[\cite{CLS}*{Prop.~5.3.9 and 5.3.10}]\label{prop:sheafmodule}
  Let $X$ be a toric variety with Cox ring $S$ and irrelevant ideal $B$.
  \begin{enumerate}
  \item Every quasicoherent sheaf on $X$ is isomorphic to $\widetilde M$ for some graded $S$-module $M$, which may be chosen finitely generated when the sheaf is coherent.
  \item If $X$ is \emph{smooth} and $M\in\Mod_{\mathrm{gr}}(S)$, then $\widetilde M=0$ if and only if $M$ is $B$-torsion.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{prop}

Consequently, when $X$ is smooth, sheafification induces an equivalence of abelian categories
\[
\Coh(X)\ \simeq\ \Mod_{\mathrm{gr}}(S)\,/\,\Mod_B(S),
\]
which is Cox's theorem \cite{cox}. Passing to derived categories yields:

\begin{thm}\label{thm:coxderived}
  Let $X=X_\Sigma$ be a smooth projective toric variety with Cox ring $S$ and irrelevant ideal $B$, and let $D^b_B(S)\subseteq D^b(S)$ be the thick subcategory of complexes with homology supported on $V(B)$. Then sheafification induces an equivalence of triangulated categories
  \[
  D^b(X)\ \simeq\ D^b(S)\,/\,D^b_B(S).
  \]
\end{thm}

This is the toric generalization of the equivalence $D^b(\PP^n)\simeq D^b(S)/D^b_\m(S)$ from Lecture~\ref{lec:3-DbPPn}; it follows from the abelian equivalence above, and is also the smooth case of Corollary~\ref{cor:DbCoxStack} below. Smoothness is essential:

\begin{example}[\cite{CLS}*{Ex.~5.3.11}]\label{ex:oddP112}
  On $\PP(1,1,2)$, let $M=\bigl(S/(x,y)\bigr)(1)$. Note that $M$ is not $B$-torsion: as $M\cong\kk[z](1)$, its support in $\bA^3$ is the entire $z$-axis, which is not contained in $V(B)=\{0\}$; concretely, $B^\ell M=z^\ell M\neq0$ for all $\ell$. Nevertheless $\widetilde M=0$. Indeed, $M$ is concentrated in \emph{odd} degrees, so on the charts of Example~\ref{ex:P112charts} we compute
  \[
  \Gamma\bigl(D_+(x),\widetilde M\bigr)=\bigl(M[x^{-1}]\bigr)_0=0, \qquad
  \Gamma\bigl(D_+(y),\widetilde M\bigr)=\bigl(M[y^{-1}]\bigr)_0=0,
  \]
  since $x$ and $y$ annihilate $M$, and
  \[
  \Gamma\bigl(D_+(z),\widetilde M\bigr)=\bigl(M[z^{-1}]\bigr)_0\cong\bigl(\kk[z^{\pm}](1)\bigr)_0=\kk[z^{\pm}]_1=0,
  \]
  not because $z$ annihilates $M$ but because $\deg(z)=2$, so the ring $\kk[z^{\pm}]$ has no odd-degree elements. Thus sheafification kills the nonzero object $M\notin D^b_B(S)$, and the induced functor $D^b(S)/D^b_B(S)\to D^b(\PP(1,1,2))$ is not an equivalence.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:simplicialsheaf}
  Example~\ref{ex:oddP112} shows that Proposition~\ref{prop:sheafmodule}(2) fails for simplicial $X$. The correct simplicial criterion is that $\widetilde M=0$ if and only if $B^\ell M_\alpha=0$ for $\ell\gg0$ and all $\alpha\in\Pic(X)$ (e.g.~see \cite[Exc.~5.3.5]{CLS}). Only the graded pieces of $M$ in \emph{Picard} degrees are visible to the variety. On $\PP(1,1,2)$ we have $\Pic(X) = 2\ZZ$ (Example~\ref{ex:P112charts}), and the module $M$ above is concentrated in odd degrees, so all of its Picard-degree pieces vanish, thus $\widetilde M=0$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Demazure vanishing}

We close with the cohomological vanishing theorem that powers the Bondal--Thomsen collection of the next lecture.

\begin{thm}[Demazure Vanishing, \cite{CLS}*{Thm.~9.2.3}]\label{thm:demazureL5}
  Let $X_\Sigma$ be a toric variety whose fan has convex support (for instance, $X_\Sigma$ complete), and let $D$ be a nef Cartier divisor on $X_\Sigma$. Then $H^p(X_\Sigma,\cO_{X_\Sigma}(D))=0$ for all $p>0$.
\end{thm}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:rounddown}
  Lecture~\ref{lec:6-bondal-thomsen} uses an extension of Theorem~\ref{thm:demazureL5} to $\QQ$-divisors: if $D=\sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$ with $a_\rho\in\QQ$ is nef, then $H^p(X,\cO_X(\lfloor D\rfloor))=0$ for all $p>0$, where $\lfloor D\rfloor=\sum_\rho\lfloor a_\rho\rfloor D_\rho$. (Note that the round-down depends on the chosen coefficients $a_\rho$, not only on the class of $D$.)  This follows by the same support-function argument; see \cite[\S9.3]{CLS} and the stronger vanishing theorems of Musta\c{t}\u{a} \cite{mustata}. It reappears as Theorem~\ref{thm:demazure} in Lecture~\ref{lec:6-bondal-thomsen}, where it yields the cohomology vanishing that characterizes Bondal--Thomsen degrees.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Frobenius pushforwards]\label{ex:frobeniussplit}
  Here is the application powering the next lecture. For an integer $m>0$, the \defi{toric Frobenius map} on a complete toric variety $X$ is the finite toric morphism $F_m\colon X\to X$ induced by multiplication by $m$ on $N$. Thomsen \cite{thomsen} showed that $F_{m*}\cO_X$ splits as a direct sum of line bundles. Since $F_m$ is finite,
  \[
  \bigoplus_{\cO(E)\,\subseteq\, F_{m*}\cO_X} H^p\bigl(X,\cO(E)\bigr) \,=\, H^p\bigl(X,F_{m*}\cO_X\bigr) \,=\, H^p\bigl(X,\cO_X\bigr) \,=\, 0 \quad\text{for } p>0,
  \]
  where the last equality is Theorem~\ref{thm:demazureL5} applied to $D=0$. Hence every line bundle summand of a toric Frobenius pushforward has vanishing higher cohomology. The finitely many line bundles arising this way form the Bondal--Thomsen collection of Lecture~\ref{lec:6-bondal-thomsen}.
\end{example}
