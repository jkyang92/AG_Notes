\section{Lecture 1 (Amal Matoo): Derived Category of $\PP^n$, I}\label{lec:1-DbPPn}
% Briefly define the bounded derived category of a variety, and briefly explain that it is an example of a triangulated category. Define the notion of a Fourier-Mukai transform between derived categories of varieties. Explain why the Fourier-Mukai transform with kernel given by the diagonal sheaf is isomorphic to the identity functor. Discuss Beilinson's resolution of the diagonal sheaf on $\PP^n\times\PP^n$.

Let $X$ be a smooth projective variety over a field $\kk$, and let $\coh X$ denote the abelian category of coherent sheaves on $X$.  The central object of these notes is the \defi{bounded derived category} $\Db(X)$, which refines the homological information carried by $\coh X$.  We briefly recall its construction and refer to \cite{huybrechts} for a thorough treatment.

\begin{defn}
  Let $K^b(X)$ denote the \defi{bounded homotopy category} of $\coh X$: its objects are bounded cochain complexes of coherent sheaves on $X$, and its morphisms are chain maps modulo homotopy.  The \defi{bounded derived category} $\Db(X)$ has the same objects as $K^b(X)$, but every quasi-isomorphism is formally inverted.  Concretely, a morphism $\cE\to \cF$ in $\Db(X)$ is represented by a \defi{roof} $\cE \xleftarrow{\ \simeq\ } \cE' \to \cF$ in which the backwards arrow is a quasi-isomorphism.
\end{defn}

Note that passing to the derived category destroys the abelian structure: kernels and cokernels no longer make sense, and there are no short exact sequences.  What survives is the weaker structure of a \defi{triangulated category}, in which short exact sequences are replaced by \defi{distinguished triangles}.

\begin{defn}
  A \defi{triangulated category} is an additive category $\cD$ equipped with an autoequivalence $E\mapsto E[1]$ (the \defi{shift}) and a class of distinguished triangles $E\to F\to G\to E[1]$ subject to a list of axioms (see \cite[\S1.2]{huybrechts}). Every morphism $f\colon E\to F$ fits into a distinguished triangle
  \[\begin{tikzcd}
    E \rar{f} & F \rar & \cone(f) \rar & E[1].
  \end{tikzcd}\]
\end{defn}

The category $\Db(X)$ is triangulated, with the shift given on a complex $\cE^\bullet$ by $\cE[1]^i=\cE^{i+1}$ and $d_{\cE[1]}=-d_{\cE}$, and with $\cone(f)^i=\cE^{i+1}\oplus \cF^i$ for a chain map $f\colon\cE^\bullet\to\cF^\bullet$.  A full subcategory closed under shifts and cones is a \defi{triangulated subcategory}; it is \defi{thick} if it is moreover closed under direct summands.  The smallest thick triangulated subcategory containing a set of objects $\{E_i\}$ is the subcategory they \defi{generate}, and we say $\{E_i\}$ \defi{generates} $\cD$ if this subcategory is all of $\cD$.

There is a fully faithful embedding $\coh X\into \Db(X)$ sending a sheaf to the complex concentrated in degree $0$, and under this embedding
\[ \Ext^i_X(\cE,\cF)\cong \Hom_{\Db(X)}(\cE,\cF[i]), \]
so that extension groups are recovered as morphisms into shifts. We henceforth identify a sheaf with the corresponding complex so that a short exact sequence $0 \to \cE \to \cF \to \cG \to 0$ in $\coh X$ is represented by a distinguished triangle $\cE \to \cF \to \cG \to \cE[1]$ in $\\Db(X)$, but note that distinguished triangles allow extensions between objects that live in different cohomological degrees, thus not every distinguished triangle comes from a short exact sequence.

\subsection{Fourier--Mukai transforms}

Any object of the derived category of a product $X\times Y$ induces an exact functor $\Db(X)\to \Db(Y)$, in a manner formally analogous to integral transforms in functional analysis.

\begin{defn}\label{defn:FMtransform}
  Let $X$ and $Y$ be smooth projective varieties, with projections
  \[
  \begin{tikzcd}[column sep=small, row sep=small]
    & X\times Y \arrow[dl,"p"'] \arrow[dr,"q"] & \\
    X & & Y.
  \end{tikzcd}
  \]
  The \defi{Fourier--Mukai transform} with \defi{kernel} $\cK\in \Db(X\times Y)$ is an exact functor
  \[ \Phi_\cK\colon \Db(X) \xto{Lp^*} \Db(X \times Y) \xto{-\Lotimes\cK} \Db(X \times Y) \xto{Rq_*} \Db(Y). \]
  where $Lp^*$, $-\Lotimes\cK$, and $Rq_*$ are the derived functors induced by $p^*$, $-\otimes\cK$, and $q_*$, respectively, but since $p$ is flat $Lp^*$ is the usual pullback.
\end{defn}

A theorem of Orlov asserts that, for smooth projective $X$ and $Y$, every exact equivalence $\Db(X)\xrightarrow{\ \simeq\ }\Db(Y)$ arises as a Fourier--Mukai transform for some kernel $\cK$ \cite{orlov_eqv}.

A special case of the Fourier--Mukai transform occurs when $Y = X$ and $\cK \in \Db(X\times X)$ is the structure sheaf $\cO_\Delta$ of the diagonal subscheme $\iota\colon\Delta\to X\times X$\!.

\begin{prop}\label{prop:diagonalidentity}
  The Fourier--Mukai transform $\Phi_{\cO_\Delta}\colon \Db(X)\to \Db(X)$ is isomorphic to the identity functor.
\end{prop}
\begin{proof}
  Write $\iota$ for the diagonal embedding, so that $p\circ\iota=q\circ\iota=\id_X$.  Using the projection formula and $\cO_\Delta=\iota_*\cO_X$, we compute
  \[
  \Phi_{\cO_\Delta}(\cE)=Rq_*\!\left(p^*\cE\Lotimes \iota_*\cO_X\right)\cong Rq_*\iota_*\!\left(\iota^*p^*\cE\right)\cong R(q\circ\iota)_*(p\circ\iota)^*\cE\cong \cE. \qedhere
  \]
\end{proof}

\subsection{Beilinson's resolution of the diagonal}
Proposition~\ref{prop:diagonalidentity} becomes a powerful computational tool once we replace $\cO_\Delta$ by an explicit locally free resolution. In this case, since $\cK$ is a complex of locally free sheaves, the left derived tensor product with $\cK$ is the usual tensor product.

On $X=\PP^n$ such a resolution was constructed by Beilinson \cite{beilinson}.  Let $\cT_{\PP^n}$ denote the tangent bundle and set
\[
\mathcal W\ce \cO_{\PP^n}(1)\boxtimes \cT_{\PP^n}(-1),
\]
a vector bundle of rank $n$ on $\PP^n\times\PP^n$, where $\cF\boxtimes\cG\ce p^*\cF\otimes q^*\cG$ denotes the external tensor product.  There is a canonical section $s\in \HH^0(\PP^n\times\PP^n,\mathcal W)$ whose vanishing locus is precisely the diagonal $\Delta$ \cite[\S8.3]{huybrechts}. \mahrud{good opportunity for an enlightning M2 computation of the actual section here} Since $\Delta$ has codimension $n=\rank\mathcal W$, the Koszul complex on $s$ is a locally free resolution
\[
0\to \textstyle\bigwedge^n\mathcal W^\vee \to \cdots\to \bigwedge^2\mathcal W^\vee\to \mathcal W^\vee \to \cO_{\PP^n\times\PP^n}\to \cO_\Delta\to 0.
\]
Using $\mathcal W^\vee\cong \cO(-1)\boxtimes \Omega^1_{\PP^n}(1)$ and $\bigwedge^i\bigl(\Omega^1(1)\bigr)\cong \Omega^i(i)$, we obtain $\bigwedge^i\mathcal W^\vee \cong \cO(-i)\boxtimes \Omega^i_{\PP^n}(i)$, so Beilinson's resolution takes the form
\[
\cO_\Delta \simeq \Big[\, \cO(-n)\boxtimes\Omega^n(n)\to \cdots\to \cO(-1)\boxtimes\Omega^1(1)\to \cO\boxtimes\cO\,\Big],
\]
a complex whose terms are finite direct sums of box products of line bundles with twisted exterior powers of the cotangent bundle. In the next lecture we combine this resolution with Proposition~\ref{prop:diagonalidentity} to prove that the line bundles $\cO,\cO(1),\dots,\cO(n)$ generate $\Db(\PP^n)$.  The same circle of ideas---now applied to resolutions of the diagonal on toric varieties---reappears in Lecture~\ref{sec:FMTransform} as the Fourier--Mukai transforms at the heart of the Cox category.
