This section is an expository introduction to the paper \cite{BCHSY}.

The inclusion of one toric variety in another is defined by a 
homogeneous \emph{pure binomial prime ideal}
in the Cox ring of the target; that is, a prime generated by elements $x^m-x^n \in S = k[x_1,\dots, x_n]$, where $m,n\in\ZZ^n$ are multi-indices. As shown in \cite{Eisenbud-Sturmfels}, 
the set $L = \{m-n\mid x^m-x^n\in I \cdot k[\ZZ]\}$ is a saturated lattice and there is a one-to-one correspondence between pure binomial ideals that are prime and saturated lattices meeting
 $\NN^n$ only in 0. Such prime ideals correspond to not-necessarily normal, affine toric varieties of embedding dimension $n$, and the ring $R \ce S/I$ is naturally graded by the group $\ZZ^n/L$.

Theorem 3.4 of \cite{BCHSY} provides a canonical $\ZZ^n/L$-graded finite $S$-free resolution $\FF$ of the normalization $\overline R$
of the ring $R$. It is obtained by factoring out a regular sequence on an $L$-equivariant $S$-free resolution $\tilde F$ 
of a certain $S$-submodule $M_L$ generated by monomials in $S[L]$.
The length of the resolution $\FF$ is equal to the 
codimension of $I$, reproving the theorem of Hochster that
$\overline R$ is Cohen-Macaulay.

The resolution $\tilde\FF$ is built from an infinite $L$-invariant cellular subdivision of $L_\RR \ce \RR\otimes L$ constructed from $L$ and the standard subdivision by coordinate planes in $\ZZ^n$, together with a ``compatible $\ZZ^n$-stratification''.  Although \cite{BCHSY} treats a more general situation, the situation they describe through the ``least common multiple stratification'' suffices for the application here, and we will describe the construction only in this special case. 

The subdivision of $L_\RR$ used by \cite{BCHSY} is defined by the coordinate hyperplanes $H_i \ce \{x\in L_R\mid x_i = 0\}$. They  divide $L_\RR$ into chambers $\sigma$ whose closures are convex polyhedra, meeting along their faces. This subdivision is preserved by the action of $L\subset \ZZ^n$, and the resulting $S$-free complex $\tilde{\FF}$ is $L$-graded. The generators of the free modules in $\tilde \FF$ correspond to
chambers in this cell complex, and the $i$-th term of the complex $\tilde\FF$ is $\oplus S\cdot\sigma$, where
the direct sum runs over all cells of dimension $i$. 

The differential of the resolution $\tilde \FF$ is obtained by choosing
an orientation on $L_\RR$ and homogenizing the usual differential of
a cell complex according to weights assigned to the chambers, as follows:
if $A$ is a cell, and $p = (p_1,\dots, p_n) \in \RR^n$ is a point in the
interior of $A$, then we give $A$ the weight
$$
\Psi(A) \ce \prod_1^n x_i^{\lceil p_i\rceil}y^{\lceil p_i\rceil}.
$$
Thus $\tilde\FF_0$ has the weights 
$$
\{\Psi(p) \mid p \hbox{ is a vertex of the cell complex } L_\RR\},
$$
so there is  an $L$-equivariant augmentation map from $\tilde\FF_0$ to
the monomial module $M_L$ generated by the weights of the vertices of the cell complex.
Proposition 3.2 of~\cite{BCHSY} shows that $\tilde \FF$ is acyclic, and is an $S$-free resolution
of $M_L$.

For $1\neq z\in L$ the element $1-z\in S[L]$ is a nonzerodivisor on any $L$-graded $S[L]$ module. 
The  resolution $\FF$ is obtained by taking the quotient of $\tilde \FF$
by the regular sequence $1-z_1, \dots 1-z_m$, where $z_1,\dots, z_m$ are the elements of $L$ that are minimal in the sense that they
are not divisible by any variables in $S$.
Proposition 3.3 of~\cite{BCHSY} shows that the quotient of the monomial module $M_L$ by $L$ is a fractional ideal of $R$ that is
isomorphic to $\overline R$. 

We now describe this resolution in the two simplest cases: when $I = (x_1-x_2)$, so that $\overline R = R$, and when $I = (x_1^3-x_2^2)$, so that $\overline R = k[x_1,x_2/x_1]$. 
To simplify the notation we write $S =k[x,y]$ in place of $k[x_1,x_2]$. Since the cellular subdivision of $L_R$ is $L$-equivariant, it suffices to consider its restriction to  a fundamental domain for the action of $L$.

The set $L_R\subset \RR^2$ meets this fundamental domain in the diagonal line. There are infinitely many 0-cells and infinitely many 1-cells, which are the $L$-translates of $a$ and $A$ respectively. We choose an (L-invariant) orientation, and take the differential to be the usual one for a cell complex, homogenized by taking the upper left corner
weight $x^0y^0=1$, the lower right corner weight $x^1y^{-1}$.
 choosing  $p= (1/2, -1/2)$ in the interior of the chamber A we give $A$ the weight
$\Psi(A) \ce \Psi(p) = x^{\lceil 1/2\rceil}yx^{\lceil -1/2\rceil} = x^1y^0$. With these choices the differential is 
$\tilde d(A) = xa-yb$. Taking the quotient by 
$L$, which identifies $b$ with $a$, we obtain the usual two-term resolution $d: S(-1)\to S$, with map  $d = (x-y)$. 
\end{example}

The action of $L$ identifies the vertices $a$ and $e$. Thus, modulo the action of $L$ and ignoring the $\ZZ^2/L = \ZZ$ grading, the  differential
of $\FF$ is
$$d: 
\bordermatrix{
     &A&B&C&D\cr
    a&x&0&0&-y\cr
    b&-1&x&0&0\cr
    c&0&-y&1&0\cr
    d&0&0&-1&x\cr    
}.
$$
This non-minimal matrix is equivalent to the direct sum of the
$2\times 2$ identity matrix and the matrix
$$
d'\ce \begin{pmatrix}
x^2 &-y\\
-y&x
\end{pmatrix}
$$
If we give $x$ and $y$ the natural weights $2$ and 3, then $d'$ is $\ZZ^2/L \cong \ZZ$-homogeneous as a map $S(-4) \oplus S(-3) \to S\oplus S(-1)$,  

The determinant of $d$ or $d'$ is easily seen to be
$(x^3-y^2)$, so the cokernel is annihilated by this element,
but the cokernel is not $k[x,y]/(x^3-y^2)$!
Since $M_L$ is generated by
$\Psi(p)$, as $p$ runs over the vertices of $L_\RR$, so $M_L/(1-(x^3/y^2))M_L$
is generated by the $\Psi(p)$ for $p$ in the fundamental domain. 
These are the fractions $1,x,x^2/y$, and (since $x^2/y = y/x$ in $R$), these generate
the fractional ideal $(1, x^2/y)  = \overline R$.

Morever, since $x^3/y = y^2/y = y$ in $R$, we have
$$
(1, x^2/y)*d' = 0
$$
and we see that the cokernel of $d'$ is also isomorphic to 
$R\cdot(x^2/y, y) = R\cdot (x^2/y)$, as required.

\section{Lecture 8 (Ryan Watson): More examples and features}
Feature includes the uniformity with respect to the Cox ring, i.e. only depending on the rays or only depending on the Cox ring.  What that means.  That tells us about resolution of graphs of birational morphisms.

\begin{cor}
\label{cor:diagonalUniform}
    Assume that $X$ and $X'$ are smooth, projective toric varieties with the same Cox ring $S$.  Then, as free complexes of $S\otimes_k S$-modules, the following are all the same:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item The resolution of the diagonal $X\subseteq X\times X$.
        \item The resolution of the diagonal $X'\subseteq X'\times X'$.
        \item  The resolution of the closure of the graph $\Gamma \subseteq X\times X'$ of the birational map $X\dashrightarrow X'$.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{cor}
\begin{proof}
    Proof 1:  The consturction of the HHL resolution only depends on the rays.  So if you do the construction in any of htese cases, it is literally exactly the same.

    Proof 2:  Write $I_\Delta\subseteq S\otimes_k S$ for the defining ideal of the diagonal $X\subseteq X\times X$.  Write  Write $I_{\Delta'}\subseteq S\otimes_k S$ for the defining ideal of the diagonal $X'\subseteq X'\times X'$.  And write $I_\Gamma\subseteq S\otimes_kS$ for the defining ideal of $\Gamma\subseteq X\times X'$.  These three ideals are all multigraded, prime ideals on $S\otimes_k S$ whose vanishing locus contains the dense torus, and thus the ideals are all equal.  The three HHL resolutions are, up to homotopy, the minimal free resolutions of the normalizations of $S\otimes_k S/I_{\Delta}$, $S\otimes_k S/I_{\Delta'}$, and $S\otimes_k S/I_{\Gamma}$ respectively.  But since these modules are all equal, so are their minimal free resolutions.
\end{proof}
Discussion of how this is kind of surprising!!  There was, a priori, no reason to believe that the ``best'' toric analogue of Beilinson's resolution  would only involve the Cox ring (i.e. that it would not depend on the irrelevant ideal whatsoever).  This is a first clue that these resolutions are pointing towards some sort of uniformity for all of the toric varieties with the same Cox ring.  That uniformity will be explored in more depth in later lectures.

\section{Lecture 9 (Greg Smith): The proof}
The original proof of the Hanlon-Hicks-Lazarev Theorem in~\cite{HHL} is fairly technical.  However, two recent preprints have dramatically simplified the proof.  The preprint~\cite{BCHSY} relates the Hanlon-Hicks-Lazarev construction to a more classical algebraic construction involving binomial ideals, and provides an elementary and purely algebraic proof of the main result, as well as broad generalization of those results.  The preprint~\cite{BH25} utilizes a similar basic idea as~\cite{BCHSY}, but provides a terser and narrower proof.
