This section is an expository introduction to the papers \cite{BCHSY} and \cite{BH25}.

The inclusion of one toric variety in another is defined by a 
homogeneous \emph{pure binomial prime ideal}
in the Cox ring of the target; that is, a prime generated by elements $x^m-x^p \in S = k[x_1,\dots, x_n]$, where $m,p\in\ZZ^n$ are multi-indices. As shown in \cite{Eisenbud-Sturmfels}, 
the set $L = \{m-p\mid x^m-x^p\in I \cdot k[\ZZ^n]\}$ is a saturated lattice and there is a one-to-one correspondence between pure binomial ideals that are prime and saturated lattices meeting
 $\NN^n$ only in 0. Such prime ideals correspond to not-necessarily normal, affine toric varieties of embedding dimension $n$. The ring $R \ce S/I$ is naturally graded by the group $\ZZ^n/L$ and has the structure of a semigroup ring $k[\NN^n/L]$ where $\NN^n/L$ is the image of $\NN^n$ under the quotient map $\ZZ^n\rightarrow \ZZ^n/L$.


Theorem 3.4 of \cite{BCHSY} provides a canonical $\ZZ^n/L$-graded finite $S$-free resolution $\FF$ of the normalization $\overline R$
of the ring $R$. It is obtained by factoring out a regular sequence on an $L$-equivariant $S$-free resolution $\tilde \FF$ 
of a certain $S$-submodule $M_L$ generated by monomials in $k[\ZZ^n]$.
The length of the resolution $\FF$ is equal to the 
codimension of $I$, reproving the theorem of Hochster that
$\overline R$ is Cohen-Macaulay.

The resolution $\tilde\FF$ is built from an infinite $L$-invariant cellular subdivision of $L_\RR \ce \RR\otimes L$. Although \cite{BCHSY} treats a more general situation, the situation they describe through the ``ceiling stratification'' suffices for the application here, and we will describe the construction only in this special case. In this special case, the subdivision of $L_\RR$ used by \cite{BCHSY} is defined by the coordinate hyperplanes $H_i \ce \{x\in L_R\mid x_i = 0\}$. They divide $L_\RR$ into chambers $\sigma$ whose closures are convex polyhedra, meeting along their faces. This subdivision is preserved by the action of $L\subset \ZZ^n$.

%% , and the resulting $S$-free complex $\tilde{\FF}$ is $L$-graded and acquires through this the structure of an $S[L]$-module. The generators of the free modules in $\tilde \FF$ correspond to
%% chambers in this cell complex, and the $i$-th term of the complex $\tilde\FF$ is $\oplus S\cdot\sigma$, where
%% the direct sum runs over all cells of dimension $i$.

The differential of the resolution $\tilde \FF$ is obtained by choosing
an orientation on $L_\RR$ and homogenizing the usual differential of
a cell complex according to weights assigned to the chambers, as follows:
if $A$ is a cell, and $p = (p_1,\dots, p_n) \in \RR^n$ is a point in the
interior of $A$, then we give $A$ the weight
$$
\Psi(A) \ce \prod_{i=1}^n x_i^{\lceil p_i\rceil}.
$$
We observe here that as the monomials assigned this way have an $L$-equivariant structure, the differentials are themselves $L$-invariant.
Thus $\tilde\FF_0$ has the weights 
$$
\{\Psi(p) \mid p \hbox{ is a vertex of the cell complex } L_\RR\},
$$
so there is  an $L$-equivariant augmentation map from $\tilde\FF_0$ to
the monomial module $M_L$ generated by the weights of the vertices of the cell complex.
Proposition 3.2 of~\cite{BCHSY} shows that $\tilde \FF$ is acyclic, and is an $S$-free resolution
of $M_L$.

That these differentials are $L$-invariant allows us to quotient by the action of $L$. Proposition 3.3 of~\cite{BCHSY} shows moreover that the quotient of $M_L$ by this $L$-action is a fractional ideal of $R$ that is isomorphic to $\overline{R}$. A more careful treatment of this quotient will be discussed in Lecture~\ref{lec:9-HHL-proof}.

The determinant of $d$ or $d'$ is easily seen to be
$(x^3-y^2)$, so the cokernel is annihilated by this element,
but the cokernel is not $k[x,y]/(x^3-y^2)$!

(1, x^2/y)*d' = 0

In the case of a resolution of a point, this use of the ceiling recovers exactly with the Bodal-Thomsen collection. In the case of a point in a toric variety $X$, the as given any cell $A$ and any element $p=(p_1,\ldots,p_n)$ of $A$ in $L_{\RR}$, the summand of the resolution $\FF$ corresponding to $A$ is generated in degree $\deg (\Psi(p))\in \Cl(X)$. This is exactly the divisor in the Bondal-Thomsen collection that is the image of $-p\in L_{\RR}$ under the map described in Definition~\ref{defn:BTcollection}. An immediate consequence is the following
\begin{cor}
  \label{cor:resPointBT}
  If $L = M_X$, then the summands in the resolution $\FF$ are exactly the Bondal-Thomsen collection
\end{cor}
\begin{proof}
  Let $A$ be a cell in $L_{\RR}$ and let $p\in \RR^n$ be a point in $A$. The summand of $\FF$ associated to the cell $A$, is $S(-\deg (\Psi(p)))=S(D)$ where
  \[D = -\sum_{i=1}^n\lceil p_i\rceil D_i=\sum_{i=1}^n\lfloor -p_i\rfloor D_i\]
  That is to say $D$ is the element of the Bondal-Thomsen collection corresponding to $-p$.
\end{proof}

The resolution depends only on the Cox ring and the lattice $L\subseteq \ZZ^n$. An interesting consequence of this is that the diagonal, and the other graphs of birational morphisms between toric varieties with the same Cox ring have the same resolution.

\begin{cor}
\label{cor:diagonalUniform}
    Assume that $X$ and $X'$ are smooth, projective toric varieties with the same Cox ring $S$.  Then, as free complexes of $S\otimes_k S$-modules, the following are all the same:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item The resolution of the diagonal $X\subseteq X\times X$.
        \item The resolution of the diagonal $X'\subseteq X'\times X'$.
        \item  The resolution of the closure of the graph $\Gamma \subseteq X\times X'$ of the birational map $X\dashrightarrow X'$.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{cor}
\begin{proof}
  Proof 1:  The construction of the HHL resolution only depends on the rays of the product and the lattice determining the diagonal. So if you do the construction in any of these cases, it is literally exactly the same.

    Proof 2:  Write $I_\Delta\subseteq S\otimes_k S$ for the defining ideal of the diagonal $X\subseteq X\times X$.  Write  Write $I_{\Delta'}\subseteq S\otimes_k S$ for the defining ideal of the diagonal $X'\subseteq X'\times X'$.  And write $I_\Gamma\subseteq S\otimes_kS$ for the defining ideal of $\Gamma\subseteq X\times X'$.  These three ideals are all multigraded, prime ideals on $S\otimes_k S$ whose vanishing locus contains the dense torus, and thus the ideals are all equal.  The three HHL resolutions are, up to homotopy, the minimal free resolutions of the normalizations of $S\otimes_k S/I_{\Delta}$, $S\otimes_k S/I_{\Delta'}$, and $S\otimes_k S/I_{\Gamma}$ respectively.  But since these modules are all equal, so are their minimal free resolutions.
\end{proof}
Discussion of how this is kind of surprising!!  There was, a priori, no reason to believe that the ``best'' toric analogue of Beilinson's resolution  would only involve the Cox ring (i.e. that it would not depend on the irrelevant ideal whatsoever).  This is a first clue that these resolutions are pointing towards some sort of uniformity for all of the toric varieties with the same Cox ring.  That uniformity will be explored in more depth in later lectures.


This uniformity also be observed in the BT collection. Observe first that the diagonal subvariety $X\subseteq X\times X$ corresponds to the lattice $L=\{(v,-v)\in M_X\,|\, v\in \}\subset \ZZ^{2n}$. This lattice is also known as the Lawrence lift of $M_X$ and can be denoted $\Lambda(M_X)$. This lattice is isomorphic to the lattice $M_X$, and this isomorphism means that the resolution of the diagonal also reflects the BT collection of $X$. More precisely
\begin{cor}
  The line bundles appearing the resolution of the diagonal $\FF$ arising from HHL are the line bundles of the form $O_X(D)\boxtimes O_X(D')$ for some $D$,$D'$ in the Bondal-Thomsen collection.
\end{cor}
\begin{proof}
  For every point $v\in M_X$, the corresponding line bundle is $O_X(-\lceil v\rceil)\boxtimes O_X(\lfloor v\rfloor)$, The left hand side of which is $O_X(-D)$ for some $D$ in the BT collection by the same reason as in Corollary~\ref{cor:resPointBT}. And $O_X(\lfloor v\rfloor)$ is quite directly the generator of the BT collection associated to $v$.
\end{proof}

\section{Lecture 9 (Greg Smith): The proof}\label{lec:9-HHL-proof}
The original proof of the Hanlon-Hicks-Lazarev Theorem in~\cite{HHL} is fairly technical.  However, two recent preprints have dramatically simplified the proof.  The preprint~\cite{BCHSY} relates the Hanlon-Hicks-Lazarev construction to a more classical algebraic construction involving binomial ideals, and provides an elementary and purely algebraic proof of the main result, as well as broad generalization of those results.  The preprint~\cite{BH25} utilizes a similar basic idea as~\cite{BCHSY}, but provides a terser and narrower proof.


Regardless of the exact approach, a key observation is that the strands of the complex $\FF$ in a fixed degree $\alpha\in \ZZ^n/L$ can be given in terms of the cellular chain complex of a space..


\begin{prop}[TODO cite both]
  \label{prop:hhlStrands}
  For $u\in \ZZ^n$, an element of the class $\alpha\in \ZZ^n/L$, the strand $\FF_{\alpha}$ is equal to the celluar homology of the subcomplex $(L_{\RR})_{\leq \alpha}$.
\end{prop}
\begin{proof}
  The complex $\FF'$ from the universal cover has strands in degree $\alpha$ given by $C_\bullet((L_{\RR})_{\leq \alpha})$.
\end{proof}

That $\FF$ is a resolution is then immediate, as the subcomplex $(L_{\RR})_{\leq \alpha}$ only has $H_0$.

Then for a semigroup ring $k[A]$, the integral closure is itself a semigroup ring, and is in fact the semigroup ring of the saturation $\overline{A}$.
