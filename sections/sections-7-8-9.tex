\section{Lecture 7 (Jason McCullough): Constructing the resolution and examples}\label{lec:7-HHL-construction}
This section is an introduction to the papers \cite{BCHSY} and \cite{BH25}.

As in Section~\ref{sec:*****}, $X = X(\Sigma)$ denotes a simplicial Toric variety where $\Sigma$ is a fan with $n$ rays, and set $M = N^{*}$. We will often make use of the exact sequence:
$$
0\to M \xrightarrow {\alpha} \ZZ^{n} \xrightarrow {deg} \Cl(X) \to 0
$$
We write $S = k[x_{1},\dots,x_{n}]= k[\NN^{n}]$ for the Cox ring of $X$.
\david{This blurb should really be near the beginning of the notes, and sections that use it (every section?) should refer to it.}


The inclusion of one toric variety in another is defined by a 
homogeneous \emph{pure binomial prime ideal}
in the Cox ring of the target; that is, a prime generated by elements $x^m-x^p \in S := k[x_1,\dots, x_n]$, where $m,p\in\ZZ^n$ are multi-indices. By \cite{Eisenbud-Sturmfels}, primeness implies that
the set $L = \{m-p\mid x^m-x^p\in I \cdot k[\ZZ^n]\}$ is a saturated lattice and there is a one-to-one correspondence between pure binomial ideals that are prime and saturated lattices meeting
 $\NN^n$ only in 0. Such binomial ideals correspond to affine, not-necessarily normal, toric varieties in $\AA^{n}$. 
 
 The ring $R \ce S/I$ is isomorphic to the semigroup ring $k[\NN^n/L]$ where $\NN^n/L$ is the image of $\NN^n$ under the quotient map $\ZZ^n\rightarrow \ZZ^n/L$.
 In the case $M = L$ then after an additional choice of irrelevant ideal, $S$ becomes the Cox ring of a projective toric variety $X$ with class group $\ZZ^n/M$, in which case $I$ is the ideal corresponding to the subvariety consisting of the identity element of the dense torus. This example will be treated in the next section.


Theorem 3.4 of \cite{BCHSY} provides a canonical  resolution $\FF$ of the normalization $\overline R$
of the ring $R$. It is obtained by factoring out a regular sequence on a $\ZZ^{n}$-graded, $L$-equivariant $S$-free resolution $\tF$
of an $S$-submodule generated by monomials in $k[\ZZ^n] \subset k(x_{1}, \dots, x_{n})$ (Definition~\ref{def:M_{L}}). Although the free modules in $\tF$ are not finitely generated, the quotient $\FF$
is a finite, $S$-free, $\ZZ^{n}/L$-graded, resolution of length equal to the 
codimension of $I$, reproving the theorem of Hochster that
$\overline R$ is Cohen-Macaulay. Moreover, the free modules $\FF_{i}$ have degrees
corresponding to the Bondal-Thomsen classes defined in the previous section. It may be easiest to
absorb the following general definitions at the same time as the special case treated in Example~\ref{first example of the HHL resolution}.


The resolution $\tF$ is built from an infinite $L$-invariant cellular subdivision of $L_\RR \ce \RR\otimes L$
and a stratification on $L_{\RR}$.  Although \cite{BCHSY} treats a more general stratification, we will
treat only the case of  the ``ceiling stratification'' and the $L$-invariant subdivision of $L_\RR$ defined by the coordinate hyperplanes $H_i \ce \{x\in L_\RR\mid x_i = 0\}$, which divide $L_\RR$ into cells $A$ whose closures are convex polyhedra, meeting along their faces; see Figure~\ref{fig1}.

%% , and the resulting $S$-free complex $\tilde{\FF}$ is $L$-graded and acquires through this the structure of an $S[L]$-module. The generators of the free modules in $\tilde \FF$ correspond to
%% chambers in this cell complex, and the $i$-th term of the complex $\tilde\FF$ is $\oplus S\cdot\sigma$, where
%% the direct sum runs over all cells of dimension $i$.

We regard the subdivision above as making $L_{\RR}$ into an infinite cell complex.
If $A$ is a cell, and $p = (p_1,\dots, p_n) \in \RR^n$ is a point in the
interior of $A$, then we give $A$ the weight \jay{If we are going to use $\ZZ^n$ for the weights can we switch to $\psi$ for consistency with \cite{BCHSY}?}
$$
\Psi(A) \ce \lceil p \rceil \in \ZZ^{n}.
$$
This is independent of the choice of $p$ because the subdivision of $L$ is induced by the coordinate hyperplanes.
 
If $B$ is 
a cell in the boundary of the closure $\overline A$, then $B$ is the intersection of
$\overline A$ with some of the hyperplanes $H_{j}$. Thus any interior point of $B$ will
be a limit of points in the interior of $A$ where some coordinates become 0. It follows that
$\Psi(B)$ will be termwise less than or equal to $\Psi(A)$; in fact $\Psi(A) - \Psi(B)$ is a vector
of 0s and 1s.

We define the term of $\tF$ corresponding to a cell $A$  to be $S(-\Psi(A))$. Thus if $p$ is an interior point of $A$
we have
\[
-\deg(\Psi(A)) = -\deg( \lceil {p}\rceil) = -\sum_{i=1}^n\lceil -p_i\rceil D_i=\sum_{i=1}^n\lfloor p_i\rfloor D_i,
\] 
which is a \BT class.

 We define the differential of the resolution $\tF$ is obtained by choosing
an orientation on $L_\RR$ and homogenizing the usual differential of
a cell complex $\partial_{L_{\RR}}A = \sum\epsilon_{i}B_{i}$, where each $\epsilon_{i} = \pm 1$, according to weights assigned to the chambers. With this homogenization, the component of the differential that maps $S(-\Psi(A))$ to $S(-\Psi(B_{i}))$ is multiplication by $\epsilon_{i}x^{\Psi(A) - \Psi(B_{i})}$. Thus the
differential preserves the $\ZZ^{n}$-grading. As the monomials assigned this way are $L$-invariant, the complex $\tF$ is $L$-invariant.


In particular,
$$
\tF_{0} = \bigoplus_{p \hbox{ is a vertex of the cell complex }L_{\RR}}S(-\Psi(p)),
$$
so there is a natural augmentation map from $\tF_{0}$ to a module $M_{L}$ generated by the corresponding monomials:

\begin{defn}\label{def:M_{L}}
 $M_{L}\subset k[\ZZ^{n}]\subset k(x_{1}, \dots, x_{n})$ is the $S$-submodule generated by the monomials
 $$
 \{x^{\Psi(p)} \mid p \hbox{ is a vertex of the cell complex }L_{\RR}\}.
 $$
\end{defn}


In Theorem~\ref{thm:HHL-resolution} we will prove that the complex $\tF$ is a resolution of $M_L$.

\begin{remark}
 Because $\Psi(A)$ is termwise $\geq \Psi(B)$ when $B$ is a vertex of $A$, the module $M_{L}$ contains (and thus is generated by) the elements $\Psi(\mathbf{p})$ for all points $\mathbf{p}\in L_{\RR}$. Thus, while the resolution $\tF$ depends on the cell structure, the module $M_L$ only depends on the lattice $L$
\end{remark}

In Theorem~\ref{thm:HHL resolution} we will prove that the complex $\tF$ is a resolution of $M_L$.
Because the differentials of $\tF$ are $L$-invariant we can form a quotient by the action of $L$, obtaining the $\ZZ^{n}/L$-graded complex $\FF$. Proposition 3.3 of~\cite{BCHSY} shows  that the quotient of $M_L$ by this $L$-action is $\overline{R}$, regarded as a fractional ideal of $R$. Putting these results together shows that  $\FF$ is a resolution of $\overline R$.

We now describe these resolutions in the two simplest cases: when $I = (x_1-x_2)$, so that $\overline R = R$, and when $I = (x_1^3-x_2^2)$, so that $\overline R = k[x_1,x_2/x_1]$. 
To simplify the notation we write $S =k[x,y]$ in place of $k[x_1,x_2]$.  

\begin{example}$R \ce k[x,y]/(x-y)$:\label{first example of the HHL resolution}
In this case the lattice $L$ is generated by the single vector $ (1,-1)\in \ZZ^2$,
which corresponds to the monomial $x/y$ if we regard $k[\ZZ^{2}]$ as a subset of $k(x,y)$.

in a single square of the lattice $\ZZ^2$, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig1}.
In this special case, the construction is the same as that of the ``hull resolution'' of \cite[Section 3]{Bayer-Sturmfels} (see also \cite[Sections 9.2--9.3]{MScomb}) of $R$.

The set $L_R\subset \RR^2$ meets this fundamental domain in the diagonal line $A$ (the dot represents a general point of $A$, which gives the label of $A$). There are infinitely many 0-cells, which are $L$-translates of $a$, and infinitely many 1-cells, which are the $L$-translates of $A$. We choose an (L-invariant) orientation, and take the differential to be the usual one for a cell complex, homogenized by taking the upper left corner $a$  
to have weight $x^0y^0=1$, the lower right corner $b$ to have weight $x^1y^{-1}$ and 
 the cell $A$ to have weight given by the interior point  $p= (1/4, -1/4)$ so that $A$ has weight
$ \Psi(p) = x^{\lceil 1/4\rceil}y^{\lceil -1/4\rceil} = x^1y^0$. With these choices $\tF$ and its augmentation to $M_{L}$ have the form
$$
\tF := 0 \to 
\tF_{1} \xrightarrow{\tilde d(A)} 
\tF_{0} \rightarrow 
M_{L} = \sum_{m\in \ZZ} S\cdot (x^{m}/y^{m}) \rightarrow 0.
$$
where
$$
\tF_{i} = \begin{cases}
  \oplus_{\ell \in L} S(\ell(A))&\hbox{ if } i = 1\\
\oplus_{\ell \in L} S(\ell(a))&\hbox{ if } i = 0\\
\end{cases}
$$
 If we write $\ell(A)$ also for the generator of $S(A)$, then The differential  $\tilde d(A) $ may be written as
$\tilde d(\ell(A)) = \sum_{\ell \in L}x^{\ell(a)}-y^{\ell(b)}$,
which makes sense because $b$ is the translate of $a$ by a generator of $L$.   
Taking the quotient by 
$L$ we obtain the usual two-term resolution $\FF: S(-1)\xrightarrow{d} S$, with map  $d = (x-y)$.

Taking the quotient of $\tF$ by $L$ is equivalent to setting the action of a generator $x^{3}/y^{2}$ of $L$ to 1, that

$$
(1, x^2/y)*d' = 0
$$
and we see that the cokernel of $d'$ is also isomorphic to 
$R\cdot(x^2/y, y) = R\cdot (x^2/y)$, as required.

\section{Lecture 8 (Ryan Watson): More examples and features}\label{lec:8-HHL-examples}

The previous lecture focused on the construction of the resolution, and through examples illustrated that for a $X'\subseteq X$ a toric subvariety with corresponding lattice $L\subseteq M$, the resolution constructed is a $\ZZ^{n}/L$ graded resolution of the normalization $\overline{R}$ of the ring $S/I_L$. The proof of this is defered to Section~\ref{lec:9-HHL-proof}. In this section, we describe in more detail the structure of the resolution as it relates to the Bondal-Thomsen classes. We start with the following summary of the resolution and the general statement on the relation to the Bondal-Thomsen collection.

\begin{cor}
  \label{cor:hhlSummandsBT}
  Let $X'\subseteq X$ be a toric subvariety,  let $L\subseteq M$ be the corresponding sublattice, and let $I_{L}$ be the corresponding binomial ideal. There is an $S$-free $\ZZ^{n}/L$-graded resolution $\FF$ of the
  normalization $\overline R$ of the ring $S/I_{L}$ such that the free modules $\FF_{i} = \oplus S(-\Psi(A))$, where the sum is over all the cells  $A$ of $L \otimes \RR$. Moreover each $S(-\Psi(A))$ is a \BT class. 
  The construction depends only on the Cox ring with its grading, and not on the additional data of the
  irrelevant ideal of the toric variety\qed
\end{cor}
\begin{proof}
  \jay{There is a key inconsistency between all of this work and Lecture 6. This section treats $M$ and $L$ as sublattices of $\ZZ^n$ whereas Lecture 6 explicitly names the inclusion $\alpha:M\rightarrow \ZZ^n$. In the correspondence, the key is that $\Psi = \lceil \alpha \rceil$. This observation is key to the arguments about BT collection elements}
  
  The proof that the complex gives a resolution of the normalization is Theorem~\ref{thm:HHL-resolution}. 
  
  Every point in $L_{\RR}$ is contained in a cell of the subdivision of $L_{\RR}$, and each cell $A$ of the subdivision
  contributes a single summand to the resolution $\FF$ of the form $S(-\deg(\Psi(A)))$.
  Thus the summands that appear in $\FF$ will be of the form $S(d)$ where $d$ is a degree in the image of the composite map $-\deg \circ \Psi$.
  %\[L_{\RR}\xrightarrow{\Psi} \ZZ^n \xrightarrow{-\deg} \Cl(X).\]

  A priori, these summands may range over all degrees in $\Cl(X)$ \jay{I don't have notation for the grading group, which may have torsion in general, but formally, these elements should be in the grading group if we want to make a distinction with $\Cl(X)$}. However, observe that as $L\subset M\subseteq \ZZ^n$, the map $\Psi:L_{\RR}\rightarrow \ZZ^n$ factors through $\RR \otimes M_X$.
  %% \[\begin{tikzcd}
  %% L_{\RR}\arrow[rr,"\Psi"] \arrow[rd,hook]& & \ZZ^n \\
  %% & \RR\otimes M_X \arrow[ru,"\Psi"]&
  %% \end{tikzcd}\]

  Consequently, the image of the composite map $-\deg \circ \Psi$ is contained in the image of $-\deg \circ \Psi$ applied to $M$. However, $-\deg \circ \Psi$ in the notation of Lecture~\ref{lec:6-bondal-thomsen} is exactly $- \deg \circ \lceil \alpha \rceil$ as the degree map is $\ZZ$-linear, we have $\deg \circ \lfloor \alpha\rfloor \circ -id$, the image of which is identical to the image of $\deg \circ \lfloor \alpha \rfloor$. By Definition~\ref{defn:BTcollection}, these are exactly the Bondal-Thomsen classes and thus the summands of $\FF$ are all \BT classes.
\end{proof}

\begin{cor}\label{cor:diagonalUniform}
If $X$ and $X'$ toric varieties with the same Cox ring $S$ then, the HHL complex of free $S\otimes_k S$-modules resolving the closed graph of the identity  $X\subseteq X\times X$,  is equal to that of the

 \end{cor}

$$
which corresponds to the sublattice 
$$
L = \{v,-v\}\mid v\in M\}\subset M\times M.
$$
 This is
the same for each of the cases in the Corollary.
\end{proof}

\section{Lecture 9 (Greg Smith): The proof}\label{lec:9-HHL-proof}
The goal of this section is to prove:

\begin{theorem} \cite{HHL}\label{thm:HHL-resolution}
With notation as in Section 7, the complexes $\tF$ and $\FF$ are $S$-free resolutions of $M_{L}$ and $\overline{S/I_{L}}$, respectively. 
\end{theorem}

The original proof of the Hanlon-Hicks-Lazarev Theorem in~\cite{HHL} is fairly technical.  However, two recent preprints have dramatically simplified the proof.  The preprint~\cite{BCHSY} (see in particular Proposition 3.2) relates the Hanlon-Hicks-Lazarev construction to a more classical algebraic construction involving binomial ideals, and provides an elementary and purely algebraic proof of the main result, as well as broad generalization of those results.  The preprint~\cite{BH25} utilizes a similar basic idea as~\cite{BCHSY}, but provides a terser and narrower proof.

Before diving into the proofs of the main theorem, it is important to carefully describe how the complexes $\tilde \FF$ and $\FF$ are related to each other and in particular provide a careful definition of the complex $\FF$.
The first step of this is to observe that as $\tilde \FF$ is $L$-equivariant, it acquires the structure of an $S[L]$-module. $S[L]$ can be presented explicitly as follows
\[S[L]=S\cdot \{z^{\mathbf{u}} \,|\, \mathbf{u}\in L\}\subseteq S[z_1^{\pm},\ldots,z_n^{\pm}]\]
$M_L$ also acquires the structure of an $S[L]$-module, where $z^{\mathbf{u}}$ acts on a monomial $x^{\mathbf{v}}\in M_L$ by sending it to $x^{\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{u}}$. It is worth observing that this makes $M_L$ into a finitely generated $S[L]$-module so long as $L\cap \NN^n=\{0\}$. It is not hard to show that the $S[L]$-module structure on $M_L$ and on $\tilde \FF$ are compatible.

Then $S$ can also be given an $S[L]$-module structure under the quotient by the ideal $\langle z^{\mathbf{u}}-1 \,|\, \mathbf{u}\in L\rangle$. Observe that for any choice of basis $\mathbf{u}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{u}_r\in L$, the sequence $z^{\mathbf{u}_1}-1,\ldots,z^{\mathbf{u}_r}-1$ forms a regular sequence for $S[L]$ and also for the module $M_L$. Then $\FF$ can be obtained from $\tilde \FF$ by the quotient by such a regular sequence, or equivalently, $\FF = \tilde \FF \otimes_{S[L]} S$.

Regardless of the exact approach, a key observation is that the strands of the complex $\FF$ in a fixed degree $\alpha\in \ZZ^n/L$ are equal to the strands $\tilde \FF_{\mathbf{u}}$ for any $\mathbf{u}\in \alpha$. Additionally both are equal to the cellular chain complex of a subcomplex of $L_{\RR}$. In particular, we can define the subset given by
\[(L_{\RR})_{\leq \mathbf{u}}:= \{\mathbf{p}\in L_{\RR} \,|\, \Psi(\mathbf{p})\text{ divides }x^{\mathbf{u}}\} = \{\mathbf{p}\in L_{\RR} \,|\, \lceil p_i\rceil \leq u_i\text{ for all } i=1,\ldots,n\}\]
Then the cellular chain complex of this subcomplex of $L_{\RR}$ is identical to the strand of $\tilde \FF$ in degree $\mathbf{u}$. This is summarized in the following proposition, the proof of which is based primarily on the proof of Proposition~2.13 and the alternate sketch of proof of Corollary~2.15 from \cite{BCHSY} and is similar to the proof of Proposition~2.6 from \cite{BH25}.

\begin{prop}[\cite{BCHSY,BH25}]
  \label{prop:hhlStrands}
  For $\mathbf{u}\in \ZZ^n$, an element of the class $\alpha\in \ZZ^n/L$, the complexes of $k$-vector spaces $\FF_{\alpha}$, $\tilde \FF_{\mathbf{u}}$ and $C_\bullet((L_{\RR})_{\leq \mathbf{u}};k)$ are isomorphic.
\end{prop}
\begin{proof}
  Interpreting $\FF$ as $\tilde \FF \otimes_{S[L]}S$ there is a map $\tilde \FF \rightarrow \FF$ by $-\otimes_{S[L]}1$. For any cell $A\subseteq L_{\RR}$, the map on the summand of $\tilde \FF$ simply is the identity map $S(-\Psi(A))\rightarrow S(-\deg(\Psi(A)))$ which coarsens the grading from $\ZZ^n$ to $\ZZ^n/L$.

  Then the image of the degree $\mathbf{u}$ component of the above is simply the map to the subspace of $S(-\deg(\Psi(A)))$ spanned by the monomial $x^{\mathbf{u}-\Psi(A)}$, or zero if the monomial is a Laurent monomial.
  Then by varying the $L$-translate $A'$ of $A$, one gets all monomials of $S(-\deg(\Psi(A)))$ of the form $x^{\mathbf{u}-\Psi(A)}$, which is a basis for the degree $\alpha$ elements of $S(-\deg(\Psi(A)))$. Applying this across all cells of $L_{\RR}/L$, one finds that the restricted map $\tilde \FF_{\mathbf{u}}\rightarrow \FF_{\alpha}$ is an isomorphism of complexes of k-vector spaces.
  
  Now to relate to the cellular homology, observe that for each cell $A\subseteq L_{\RR}$ the summand of $A$ in $\tilde \FF$ has component in degree $\mathbf{u}$ given by

  \[S(-\Psi(A))_{\mathbf{u}} =
  \begin{cases}
    k & \Psi(A)\text{ divides } x^{\mathbf{u}}\\
    0 & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}\]

  Thus the cells of $L_{\RR}$ for which the summand corresponding to $A$ participates are exactly those cells contained in $(L_{\RR})_{\leq \mathbf{u}}$. Moreover, the maps between these summands are exactly the differentials from the cellular structure, so we have
  \[\tilde \FF_{\mathbf{u}} \cong C_\bullet((L_{\RR})_{\leq \mathbf{u}};k)\]
\end{proof}

That $\FF$ is a resolution is then immediate, as the subcomplexes of the form $(L_{\RR})_{\leq \mathbf{u}}$ are polyhedral and thus contractible or empty. As a consequence, the only homology is in $H_0$.

The second key is to prove that the module is the integral closure $\overline{S/I_L}$. Here we recall a fact about toric rings. First the ring $S/I_L$ is a semigroup ring for the semigroup $A=\NN^n/L$, that is the image of $\NN^n$ under the map $\ZZ^n \rightarrow \ZZ^n/L$. Then for a semigroup ring $k[A]$, the integral closure is itself a semigroup ring, and is in fact the semigroup ring of the saturation $\overline{A}$.

The saturation of an affine semigroup $A\subseteq \ZZ^n$ is given by
\[\overline{A} := (\RR_{\geq 0}A)\cap \ZZ^n \]

There are then two plausible approaches to proving this, which correspond to the approaches from \cite{BCHSY} and \cite{BH25} respectively. One can either prove that the generators of the module $M_L$ correspond to monomials in $k[\ZZ^n]$ which generate $\overline{A}$ as an $A$-module. Or one can prove that those degrees $\alpha\in \ZZ^n/L$ where $(L_{\RR})_{\leq \mathbf{u}}$ is non-empty are exactly the degrees of $A$. The approach taken here follows mostly the approach in Proposition~3.3 of \cite{BCHSY}. The corresponding result in \cite{BH25} is Lemma~2.8.

\begin{prop}[\cite{BCHSY,BH25}]
  \label{prop:resolves-integral-closure}
  $M_L \otimes_{S[L]} S$ is isomorphic to $\overline{S/I_L}$ as an $S$-module.
\end{prop}
\begin{proof}
  $M_L\otimes_{S[L]} S$ is generated by monomials in $k[\ZZ^n/L]$ of the form $x^{\lceil \mathbf{v}\rceil}$ for $\mathbf{v}$ a vertex of the cellular structure on $L_{\RR}$\jay{TODO technically this needs the regular sequence statement/the exactness of the functor}. And the normalization viewed as the semigroup ring $k[\overline{A}]$ is naturally an $S$-submodule of $k[\ZZ^n/L]$. So we will show that they are identical as submodules.

  First we show that the monomials $x^{\lceil \mathbf{v}\rceil}$ are elements of the semigroup $\overline{A}$.
  Let $\eta:\ZZ^n\rightarrow \ZZ^n/L$ be the usual quotient map and $\eta_{\RR}:\RR^n\rightarrow \RR^n/L$ the corresponding map after tensor with $\RR$. For a vertex $\mathbf{p}\in L_{\RR}$, we have
  \[\eta(\lceil \mathbf{p}\rceil) = \eta(\lceil \mathbf{p}\rceil)-\eta_{\RR}(\mathbf{p}) = \eta(\lceil \mathbf{p}\rceil - \mathbf{p})\]
  However $\lceil \mathbf{p}\rceil - \mathbf{p}$ has all non-negative coordinates and so is in $\RR_{\geq 0} A=\eta(\RR_{\geq 0}^n)$.

  For the converse, suppose that $\mathbf{a}\in \overline{A}$, then
  we wish to show that the corresponding element of $k[\overline{A}]$ is an element of $M_L\otimes_{S[L]} S$.
  As $\mathbf{a}\in \overline{A} = \eta(\RR_{\geq 0}^n)\cap (\ZZ^n/L)$, there exists
  $\mathbf{q}\in \RR_{\geq 0}^n$ and $\mathbf{u}\in \ZZ^n$ such that
  \[\mathbf{a} = \eta_{\RR}(\mathbf{q}) = \eta(\mathbf{u})\]

  It suffices to prove there exists a vertex $\mathbf{p}'\in L_{\RR}$ such that $\Psi(\mathbf{p}')$ divides $x^{\mathbf{u}}$.
  Let $\mathbf{p}=\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{q}$, then as $\lceil \mathbf{p} \rceil = \mathbf{u} - \lfloor \mathbf{q}\rfloor$ and $\mathbf{q}\in \RR_{\geq 0}^n$ it follows that $\Psi(\mathbf{p})$ divides $x^{\mathbf{u}}$. To get a vertex, observe that if $\mathbf{p}'$ is any vertex of the unique cell of $L_{\RR}$ that contains $\mathbf{p}$ in its relative interior, then $\Psi(\mathbf{p}')$ divides $\Psi(\mathbf{p})$ and thus divides $x^{\mathbf{u}}$ as desired.
\end{proof}

Together these two propositions prove Theorem~\ref{thm:HHL-resolution}.

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:HHL-resolution}]
  That the complexes $\tilde \FF$ and $\FF$ are resolutions follows from Proposition~\ref{prop:hhlStrands} by observing that $(L_{\RR})_{\leq \mathbf{u}}$ is polyhedral and thus either contractible or empty.

  That the complex $\tilde \FF$ resolves $M_L$ follows first by observing that there exists a canonical $\ZZ^n$-graded map $\tilde \FF_0\rightarrow k[\ZZ^n]$ compatible with the resolution, thus giving a $\ZZ^n$-graded map $H_0(\tilde \FF)\rightarrow k[\ZZ^n]$.
  To show that $H_0(\tilde \FF)\cong M_L$, observe first that for a degree $\mathbf{u}\in \ZZ^n$,
  $H_0(\tilde \FF)_{\mathbf{u}}=H_0((L_{\RR})_{\leq \mathbf{u}})$. Moreover $(L_{\RR})_{\leq \mathbf{u}}$ is contractible if there exists a vertex $\mathbf{v}\in L_{\RR}$ such that $\Psi(\mathbf{v})$ divides $x^{\mathbf{u}}$, and empty otherwise. As $M_L$ is generated by the vertices $\mathbf{v}\in L_{\RR}$, it follows that $(L_{\RR})_{\leq \mathbf{u}}$ is contractible if and only if $x^{\mathbf{u}}\in M_L$ and empty otherwise.

  That the complex $\FF$ resolves $\overline{S/I_L}$ follows from Proposition~\ref{prop:resolves-integral-closure}.
\end{proof}
